# Manuscript Structure

Use this file to plan or revise sports biomechanics manuscripts.

## Title

- Name the population, task, exposure/intervention, and key outcome when possible.
- Avoid vague titles such as "A biomechanical analysis of..." unless the design is truly exploratory.
- Prefer searchable terms used by the target journal's audience.

## Abstract

Use a compact structure:

- Background: one sentence on the unresolved problem.
- Purpose: the precise aim.
- Methods: design, participants, task, instruments, primary variables, and statistics.
- Results: quantified primary findings.
- Conclusion: one defensible implication tied to the data.

Do not include broad claims unsupported by the study design.

## Introduction

Paragraph 1: establish the applied or theoretical problem.

Paragraph 2: synthesize what is known, focusing on mechanisms and evidence quality rather than listing papers.

Paragraph 3: identify the precise gap. Good gaps are about unknown mechanisms, inconsistent evidence, inadequate methods, untested populations, or unclear practical translation.

Final paragraph: state aim, hypotheses, and expected contribution.

## Methods

Make the study reproducible. Use subheadings such as:

- Study design
- Participants
- Experimental protocol
- Instrumentation
- Data processing
- Outcome variables
- Statistical analysis

Report enough detail that another biomechanics lab could reproduce the experiment and analysis.

## Results

- Start with participant flow and descriptive characteristics when relevant.
- Present primary outcomes first, then secondary and exploratory outcomes.
- Report estimates with uncertainty, not only p values.
- Keep interpretation out of the results unless the target journal explicitly favors integrated results and discussion.

## Discussion

Paragraph 1: answer the research question with the main findings, avoiding a repeat of all results.

Middle paragraphs: interpret mechanisms, compare with prior work, explain disagreements, and discuss practical meaning.

Limitations: focus on limitations that affect interpretation, such as sample specificity, task constraints, measurement error, model assumptions, and unmeasured confounders.

Final paragraph: state the contribution and next step without overstating causality.

## Common Reviewer Triggers

- "Novel" without a specific field-level contribution.
- Methods missing filter settings, coordinate definitions, event definitions, or normalization.
- Too many outcomes without pre-specified primary variables.
- Causal language from observational or acute laboratory data.
- Clinical or performance recommendations not supported by the sample or task.
